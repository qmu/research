# LLM Response Speed: Sustained Throughput, TTFT, and Total Latency

The single clearest decision-relevant finding is that **time-to-first-token, not sustained throughput, dominates perceived responsiveness for short interactive exchanges**, and the two metrics are largely decoupled across vendors and effort levels. GPT-5.4 nano at "high" effort measured a 467ms TTFT with 165 tokens/sec throughput and a 596ms total latency for a short prompt — while Claude Fable 5 at "max" effort measured a 5,842ms TTFT despite a comparable 130.7 tokens/sec throughput once generation started. A model can be "fast" in one dimension and "slow" in the other; buyers optimizing for chat-like UX should weight TTFT heavily, while those optimizing for bulk generation (long documents, batch pipelines) should weight sustained throughput instead.

A second consistent pattern: **raising reasoning/effort level frequently increases latency without proportionally improving — and sometimes actively degrades — instruction-following accuracy**, which complicates any simple "spend more compute, get better output" assumption. Claude Sonnet 5 at "max" effort recorded a 16,739ms TTFT and 0% length accuracy, versus 792ms TTFT and 93% accuracy at "low" effort — a case where higher effort produced both a 20x latency penalty and worse constraint adherence in this single trial. GPT-5.3 Codex at "xhigh" effort recorded 0 tokens/sec sustained throughput (the model appears to have stalled mid-generation, per the truncated raw output) alongside a 2,430ms TTFT. These are single-trial observations, not stable characterizations of effort-scaling behavior, but they suggest effort/reasoning sliders should be validated empirically per-task rather than assumed monotonic.

On raw speed, **smaller/cheaper tier models were frequently faster on both axes than flagship siblings from the same vendor**, reversing a naive "you get what you pay for" heuristic for latency specifically. GPT-5.4 nano and GPT-5.4-mini posted throughput figures above 140–165 tokens/sec with sub-second TTFT at several effort levels, and Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite reached 250–460 tokens/sec — all well above their respective flagship counterparts (Gemini 3.1 Pro's TTFT ranged 4.2–7.1 seconds; GPT-5.5's throughput stayed in the 28–38 tokens/sec band). This is a real speed/capability trade-off: the artifact doesn't measure task accuracy directly (only length-instruction adherence and schema depth/breadth as structural proxies), so faster small models may sacrifice reasoning quality not captured by these speed metrics.

Two clear reliability caveats limit how far these numbers should be trusted. First, **several configurations show anomalous or degenerate results that are measurement artifacts, not durable performance characteristics** — GPT-5.3 Codex xhigh reported 0 tokens/sec with a truncated response; o4-mini "high" and Claude Sonnet 5 "max" show length accuracy of 0%; several rows show huge TTFT outliers (7–52 seconds) that likely reflect queuing, cold starts, or provider-side variance rather than steady-state latency. These should be read as "observed once" rather than "typical." Second, **schema depth/breadth figures are capped or configuration-dependent** (e.g., probe caps of depth 48 / breadth 192) and a small maxSchemaBreadth or depth in a given row (like o4-mini "high" showing breadth of only 2) reflects what the model actually produced in that trial, not a hard architectural limit — these are not comparable across rows without checking whether the model hit the probe's ceiling or stopped short of it.

Overall, this artifact represents **exactly one trial per model/effort configuration**, with no repeated sampling, no reported variance, and no controlled load conditions — all stdDev values are zero because n=1. Given LLM API latency's well-known sensitivity to load, geography, and time-of-day, none of the specific millisecond or token-per-second figures here should be treated as representative averages; they are single-sample snapshots useful for spotting gross patterns and trade-offs (e.g., TTFT vs. throughput decoupling, effort-level non-monotonicity) but not for fine-grained ranking of models within a few percentage points of each other. Any procurement or architecture decision should be validated with repeated, production-representative trials before being finalized.
