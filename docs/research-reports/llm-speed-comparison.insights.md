# LLM Response Speed: Sustained Throughput, TTFT, and Total Latency

The single clearest decision-relevant finding is that **time-to-first-token, not sustained throughput, dominates total response time for typical short-to-medium interactions** — and TTFT varies enormously and often unpredictably across models, providers, and even effort levels within the same model. GPT-5.4 nano at high effort measured a 467ms TTFT with 165 tokens/sec throughput and a 596ms total latency for the short-answer probe, while Grok Build 0.1 measured a 9,531ms TTFT (9.68s total latency) despite respectable 227 tokens/sec throughput once generation began. Gemini 3.1 Pro (low effort) and GPT-5.3 Codex (xhigh) similarly showed multi-second TTFT (6.5s and 2.4s respectively) that swamped any advantage from raw token generation speed. This suggests that for latency-sensitive, interactive use cases (chat, autocomplete, agentic loops), TTFT is the metric to optimize for, and it is not reliably predicted by model tier, provider, or price.

A second observation worth separating from judgment: **higher "effort" or reasoning settings do not consistently trade speed for quality in a predictable direction** — sometimes they roughly double both TTFT and total latency (Claude Opus 4.8 low→xhigh: 1237ms→2143ms TTFT), sometimes they produce extreme outliers (Claude Sonnet 5 at max effort: 16,739ms TTFT, and its xhigh setting scored 0% length-instruction accuracy in this single trial), and sometimes higher effort actually *improves* speed (GPT-5.4 nano's high effort was faster than its "none" effort in this sample). Given the trial count is n=1 per configuration, these effort-level swings could reflect genuine architectural behavior or simply noise/queueing variance on the measurement day — the artifact cannot distinguish the two, and no stdDev or repeated-trial data exists to bound confidence.

On sustained throughput, the small/nano-tier models were frequently faster than flagship models — GPT-5.4 nano (high effort) hit 165 tokens/sec and GPT-5.1 Codex mini (medium effort) hit 219 tokens/sec, both well above most frontier-tier figures like Claude Opus 4.8 (~60-66 tokens/sec) or GPT-5.5 (~28-38 tokens/sec). This is a genuine speed/cost/capability trade-off: the small models are also 10-50x cheaper per output token (e.g., GPT-5.4 nano at $0.60/MTok output vs. GPT-5.5 at $30/MTok), making them attractive where raw throughput and cost matter more than the deeper reasoning or larger schema handling seen in some flagship configurations. Notably, schema-handling capacity (max depth/breadth reached) also varies by provider independent of speed — Grok 4.3 and Grok 4.20 reached schema depths of 48, versus GPT-5.x models capped around depth 10 — so buyers optimizing purely for speed should check whether a fast small model still meets their structured-output complexity needs.

Reliability of instruction-following (length accuracy) is a second axis that sometimes anti-correlates with speed in ways that matter for selection: Claude Sonnet 5 at xhigh effort was fast (961ms TTFT, ~90 tokens/sec) but scored 0% length accuracy, and its max-effort configuration combined a 16.7-second TTFT with 0% length accuracy — the worst of both dimensions simultaneously in this sample. This underscores that speed metrics alone are insufficient for model selection; a fast, cheap response that ignores explicit constraints may cost more in downstream correction than a slower, compliant one.

Finally, these figures should be read as a snapshot, not a statistically robust benchmark: **every cell in this artifact represents a single trial (n=1) per model/effort/provider combination**, with reported stdDev of 0 only because no repeat trials were run to reveal variance. Real-world API latency is known to fluctuate with load, region, and time of day, and several data points here look like plausible outliers (e.g., o4-mini high effort's 7.4s TTFT versus its low-effort 930ms, or GPT-5.3 Codex xhigh's malformed 0 tokens/sec throughput reading). Treat this report as a directional comparison and a methodology template — not as a certified production SLA — and prioritize re-running the sweep with multiple trials per configuration before making binding infrastructure commitments.
